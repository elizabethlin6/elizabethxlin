Wrapping Up My First CS Class June 8, 2018

An energetic version of me three months ago in the start of the Spring Quarter sat on my desk at the weak hour of 2pm, happy after successfully slipping into a high-demand data programming class at UW called CSE 160. In the span of a year, I have developed lasting friendships, not only with my laptop, but with a small group of TAs and hard-working students that I call my debugging companions.


CSE 160 is listed on the UW course website as "Data Programming" - and data programming was sure what I learned in the span of the last ten weeks. Coming with vague Computer Science knowledge, I was introduced to the sleek, straight-forward coding language of Python. The first week, I was successively understanding the arithmetic involved in the coding process, but as the weeks progressed, I fell into a pit whole of self doubt.


Every week, there was a new challenging assignment that made me go to every office hours to get assistance and clarity. I would say forty percent of my time spent during the day was dedicated to going to office hours on the fifth floor of the CSE building. Looking back, these vital hours spent were places where I developed lasting friendships, greater insight on what it takes to be a "computer scientist," and challenge beyond my known limits.


The TAs were a massive support and inspiration for my continual motivation throughout the quarter to persevere through the class. They taught me to be resilient and open-minded when writing code, usually involving parsing the problem on the white board before trying to write the code. When writing test cases, it is also important to recognize what data structure you are using and what output you are trying to make (like a nested dictionary, list of dictionaries). But these strategies didn't come easy for me, and that is why I value the supportive staff including Professor Anderson and the knowledgable TAs who strategize different ways for me to think dynamically about the given problem and route me toward the right approach. It was also through the staff that I began to understand the underlying importance of learning Computer Science for the future of tech and innovation in the world. Learning about the labs and on-campus activities that the TAs were involved in, inspired me to seek ways to practice my coding skills with others.


There is an art that in the late hours of turning a homework assignment or final project that you befriend students who share your similar frustrations and struggles. This was the case for me where I (basically) spent three days camping in the CSE building, arriving at 10am and leaving at 10pm to work on my project. Since every student had a different project of their own, it was a very unique and positive community that I felt in my interactions with other students. I loved hearing about the interesting projects that students were working on, from analyzing NBA players' salaries to weather patterns in -- . Even though going to office hours felt like a 12 hours job (it really was in the last days of the class), it proved to be a fun, lasting memory to bond with a group of talented and diverse students and learn about their projects and giving each other feedback.


From blurring images using pixel grids to detecting election fraud in various countries' elections, the homework assignments made me realize how applicable computer science can be in any field. In fact, learning about the immediate results that can be obtained by analyzing large datasets, writing testable algorithms motivated me to pursue projects of my own. This summer, I plan to work on practicing my Python skills by analyzing large datasets and explore more applications of Python in Arduino projects (I really want to make a hat that lights up with Python!)


To end my favorite class of freshman year, I would like to say I always think the first impression of any thing, especially a course, can either attract students toward the field or have them running away from it. I believe in my case, there were moments where I felt like I was incapable of the skills that I have right now. But thanks to a unique and supportive community in CSE 160 that I am now coming out as a confident data scientist with many more data analyzing skills to master and learn. This class is no different, as I came in like a kindergardener with no knowledge of the alphabet, but now I am equipped with the very fundamental knowledge of the Alphabet (in this case Python) and guidance of expanding my coding skills. I plan to cherish this experience to create more informational projects that can better educate others about the big data out there in the world.
