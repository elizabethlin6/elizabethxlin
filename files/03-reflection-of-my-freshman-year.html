Reflection of my Freshman Year June 8, 2018

Today, I realized that it is hard to come by college instructors who can help solidify your fundamental understanding in any field. Coming into the STARS Program in the fall, I was hesitant to accept my fate: commitment to a two-year structured engineering curriculum. I wanted autonomy. I wanted to be independent from my family, friends, and discover my potential in an environment different from high school.


My prologue to college began on an early September evening where I had finally moved all of my stuff into my dorm. I lived in the Engineering Living Learning Community in Maple Hall, a normally a bustling avenue of busy STEM students. With specks of dust showering my clothes from moving furniture, I checked the time: 5:50pm. Alertedly, I grabbed my STARS summer homework and rushed down into the Alder Auditorium, a building two blocks from Maple Hall. It was there, at the exact time 6:00pm where the start of my college career began - the beginning of STARS Transition Week. Rushing into a room of unfamiliar faces, little did I know this small group of students would become my college family.


Being in the STARS Engineering Program, I realized in my autumn quarter, was no easy task. With two three-hour tutoring sessions a week, a STARS seminar class, math workshops, and weekend MTUDs (Math Til You Drop: an eight hour math workshop prior to midterm/finals week where we work a packet of math problem until we - quite literally - drop.), I barely had any personal time to meet other students outside of the STARS community. But within STARS, I befriended long-lasting friends who supported me academically and personally. We were a cohort of diverse engineering students who were training to become master engineers. In the first couple of weeks, college really slapped us in the face. We stayed up long nights to finish STARS Math engineering homework, decorate Maple Hall Study Lounges with beautiful sinusoidal graphs, and bonded through corny math jokes and free food. It was a gift to have such a tight-knit community to constantly help each other, but the bridge from fall quarter to winter quarter, I made it my goal to get more involved with campus extracurricular activities.



In winter quarter, I decided to join an IMA Womens Basketball League team and was invited to become an events coordinator for HuskyTech. I enjoyed getting involved with new activities, because it gave me the chance to meet more people and learn from their experiences. I especially loved the HuskyTech community, where I was introduced to passionate and motivated students interested in Tech. The mission of the club was to “Accelerate your Tech Career.” As an event coordinator, I was constantly talking to industry professionals, event-sponsors, and team executives to ensure every aspect of our events were top-quality. This opportunity helped me to build connections with people I wouldn’t have met or even have the chance to talk to. Winter quarter was a chapter of new beginnings, as the people I surrounded myself with in HuskyTech and STARS made me a motivated, proactive, and engaged student.



While balancing the rigorous STARS Chemistry and MATH 124 class, I found myself on a race with time. There was so many things I wanted to do, yet time always seemed to fall short. So I attempted another strategy Spring Quarter:

Dedicating time doing things that I liked and being very good with my time management.






I found two jobs that I loved in the spring: interning at Fred Hutchinson Cancer Research Center in a Biostatistics lab investigating breast cancer reocurrence using data science and developing Java and Python curriculum for summer camps for Computing Kids. Despite my regular school schedule, I felt like every week had a different dynamic and I would come out of the week learning something more than I could learn in a classroom setting. The time crunch made me even more productive than before, as there was no room for procrastination. I set weekly goals for myself, which spanned from remembering to submit my STARS workshop assignments to making sure to grab my favorite lunch at Local Point’s Tero.



Spring quarter was my real independent journey, as I did not share many classes with other STARS students except our math classes. I was enrolled in a Data Programming class in Python (CSE 160) where I challenged myself to adapt to new coding syntax. The fast-paced course made sure I was present at office hours every week to ask TAs infinitesimal questions about coding logic and structure. With each homework assignment getting progressively difficult, I befriended students who shared the same frustrations and determination to persevere through together on each weekly assignment. After wrapping up the class last week with a final project presentation, I realize this class was probably my favorite freshman class because of all the valuable skills it equipped me with in the span of ten weeks. Coupled with my data science internship at Fred Hutch, I felt more prepared than ever to embark this summer break to work on my personal projects involving data analysis (In fact, I wrote a very short program on the NBA Finals - keep in mind I am a Cavaliers fan! You can see it here.)


Freshman year has been a big learning experience. But I hope to utilize this chapter of my life as a stepping stone towards building my goals and shaping my personal values for the future. I look forward this summer to interning at local tech startups while working on my own side projects. Looking back a year from now, its crazy to reflect on the massive amount of things I have learned about myself and my peers. I would like to thank all the people who have supported me in my first year of college. I look forward to keep you updated on my future endeavors. Thank you.
